1. What is Cloud Computing?

In simple terms, cloud computing is the **on-demand delivery of IT resources**—like servers, storage, databases, networking, software, and analytics—over the internet (the "cloud"). Instead of owning and maintaining physical data centers and servers, you access technology services from a cloud provider (like AWS, Azure, or Google Cloud) on a pay-as-you-go basis.

The Top 3 Benefits for Your Business

  • **Cost Savings:** Pay only for the resources you consume. No capital expenditure on hardware.
  • **Scalability:** Instantly scale resources up or down in minutes, not months.
  • **Global Reach:** Deploy your application to users worldwide quickly and efficiently.

2. The Service Models: IaaS, PaaS, SaaS

Cloud services are typically categorized by the level of management provided by the vendor versus the level managed by the client. Think of it as **pizza as a service**:

IaaS

Infrastructure as a Service

What it is: Provides the building blocks: networking, virtual machines (VMs), storage. You manage the OS, applications, and runtime.
Analogy: Buying raw pizza dough and ingredients (You manage the cooking).

PaaS

Platform as a Service

What it is: Provides an environment for developing, running, and managing applications without the complexity of infrastructure management.
Analogy: Buying a pre-made pizza base and sauce (You focus on the toppings and serving).

SaaS

Software as a Service

What it is: Delivers software applications over the internet, typically on a subscription basis (e.g., Gmail, Salesforce).
Analogy: Ordering a fully prepared, delivered pizza (You only eat it).

Concept Checkpoint

If you need to deploy a custom application but don't want to manage the underlying operating system, which service model should you choose?

Answer: **PaaS** (Platform as a Service).

3. Deployment Models: Where Does the Cloud Live?

Not all clouds are the same. Your security, compliance, and budget requirements will determine the best fit:

  • Public Cloud: Owned and operated by a third-party cloud service provider. All hardware, software, and other supporting infrastructure is owned and managed by the provider. (Most common choice).
  • Private Cloud: Cloud resources used exclusively by a single business or organization. It can be physically located on your company's premises or hosted by a third-party.
  • Hybrid Cloud: A combination of two or more distinct cloud infrastructures (private and public) that remain unique entities but are bound together by technology that enables data and application portability.
