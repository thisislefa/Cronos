7 Common IT Security Scams and How to Avoid Them

Ever heard of **social engineering** attacks and **malware distribution**? Be smarter than the attackers and learn how common IT security breaches work in detail.

The **US Cyber Command** (CYBERCOM) estimates that, between January and March 2025, more than US$1.3 billion in corporate data and credentials were stolen by cybercriminals. It is a significant amount, given that by mid-2025, the market cap of all combined cybersecurity firms sat just below US$1 trillion.

In this article, we discuss seven common IT security threats, how they work, and precautionary measures you can take to protect your organization's assets and intellectual property.

Key Takeaways:

  • **Phishing/Spear Phishing** can involve messaging employees with malicious links impersonating internal IT or management.
  • **Ransomware** reaches systems via email attachments or unpatched vulnerabilities, encrypting data until a ransom is paid.
  • **DDoS Attacks** require botnets to flood a target's server with traffic to cause service disruption.
  • **Supply Chain Attacks** target third-party vendors or software dependencies to gain access to a large network of users.
  • **SQL Injection** exploits poorly validated input fields to execute unauthorized database queries.
  • **Man-in-the-Middle** (MITM) attacks intercept communication between two parties to steal data.
  • **Zero-Day Exploits** leverage unknown vulnerabilities before security patches are available.

1. Understanding Phishing and Social Engineering

Phishing remains one of the most effective ways for attackers to gain initial access. Attackers leverage human psychology—specifically trust and urgency—to trick employees into giving up credentials. This could be a fake email about an expired password or an urgent request from the "CEO" to transfer funds.

2. Ransomware and Data Loss Prevention

Ransomware attacks have grown exponentially. The only true defense is a robust backup and recovery plan that adheres to the 3-2-1 rule: three copies of data, two different media types, and one copy offsite. Continuous education for employees is also paramount.

3. The Threat of Zero-Day Exploits

A zero-day exploit is a software vulnerability that is unknown to the vendor. Because no patch exists, they are incredibly dangerous. Defense strategies rely heavily on robust network monitoring, behavioral analysis, and rapid patching once a fix is released.
